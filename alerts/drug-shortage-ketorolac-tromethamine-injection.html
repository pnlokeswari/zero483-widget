Its non-addictive nature makes it particularly crucial in an era dominated by the opioid crisis, offering an effective alternative for acute pain without the risks of dependence and abuse associated with narcotics. This makes its current shortage not just a supply issue, but a challenge to national efforts to curb opioid over-prescription and improve safer pain management practices.

Manufacturing complexities, reliance on single source suppliers for active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs), and economic pressures on generic drug manufacturers all contribute to this precarious situation. When a drug like Ketorolac, with its broad utility in emergency medicine, surgery, and general acute care, becomes scarce, it underscores the systemic vulnerabilities that healthcare providers and patients face daily.

The FDA plays a critical role in tracking and responding to drug shortages, working closely with manufacturers to prevent new shortages, mitigate existing ones, and expedite reviews of alternative products. For healthcare systems, a shortage like Ketorolac's means adapting protocols on the fly, investing in more expensive or less familiar alternatives, and ensuring staff are adequately trained on new treatment pathways.

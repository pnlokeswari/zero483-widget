Urgent Recall: Lacosamide Tablets by Annora Pharma & Camber Pharmaceuticals — What Patients Need to Know About Potential Selexipag Mix-Up

Published: 2026-06-04  |  Severity: High  |  Source: Original Publication

Article Analysis & Summary

A critical pharmaceutical safety alert has been issued by Annora Pharma Private Limited, announcing a nationwide Class II recall of Lacosamide Tablets, USP, 100mg. This essential medication, primarily prescribed to manage partial-onset seizures, is being recalled due to a serious concern: the potential presence of foreign tablets—specifically, Selexipag 1000 mcg tablets—within bottles intended solely for Lacosamide. This recall impacts 31,392 60-count bottles, distributed across the USA, under NDC 31722-813-60, manufactured by Annora Pharma Pvt., Ltd., and distributed for Camber Pharmaceuticals, Inc. The status of this recall is ongoing, underscoring the immediate need for vigilance among patients and healthcare providers alike.

The gravity of this recall cannot be overstated. Lacosamide is an anti-epileptic drug (AED) crucial for preventing seizures, and inconsistent dosing or sudden discontinuation can lead to breakthrough seizures, status epilepticus (prolonged seizures that are a medical emergency), or other severe neurological complications. Compounding this risk, the foreign tablet identified, Selexipag 1000 mcg, is a potent medication used to treat Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension (PAH)—a life-threatening condition affecting blood vessels in the lungs. Accidental ingestion of Selexipag by someone not suffering from PAH could lead to dangerously low blood pressure (hypotension), dizziness, fainting, headaches, and potentially more severe cardiovascular events, especially given its high potency.

The USFDA's classification of this as a Class II recall indicates that the use of or exposure to the recalled product may cause temporary or medically reversible adverse health consequences, or where the probability of serious adverse health consequences is remote. However, in this specific instance, the direct exchange or co-ingestion of two drugs with such divergent and potent pharmacological profiles elevates the risk considerably. A patient missing their essential seizure medication while simultaneously taking a powerful vasodilator designed for a different, serious condition presents a unique and immediate threat to patient safety.

This recall highlights the absolute imperative for stringent quality control in pharmaceutical manufacturing and distribution. For individuals managing epilepsy, the integrity of their medication supply is paramount to their health and well-being. This incident serves as a stark reminder for patients to meticulously check their medications and for healthcare systems to implement robust verification processes.

Context & Background

Lacosamide, sold under brand names like Vimpat, is a widely prescribed anti-epileptic drug (AED) used as an adjunctive or monotherapy treatment for partial-onset seizures in patients four years of age and older. Partial-onset seizures originate in one part of the brain and can manifest with a variety of symptoms, from altered awareness to involuntary movements. For individuals living with epilepsy, consistent access to accurate, potent medication is not just about managing symptoms; it's about maintaining stability, preventing life-threatening events, and improving overall quality of life. Any interruption in therapy or substitution with an incorrect drug can have immediate and severe consequences, underscoring the critical nature of this recall.

On the other side of this mix-up is Selexipag, known by its brand name Uptravi. This medication belongs to a class of drugs called prostacyclin receptor agonists and is specifically approved to delay disease progression and reduce the risk of hospitalization for pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH). PAH is a rare, progressive, and often fatal disease characterized by high blood pressure in the arteries leading to the lungs, forcing the heart to work harder. Selexipag works by relaxing and widening these blood vessels, significantly reducing blood pressure in the lungs. While life-saving for PAH patients, its potent vasodilatory effects mean that accidental ingestion by someone without PAH could lead to severe, potentially dangerous drops in systemic blood pressure, especially in patients who might also be on other medications that affect blood pressure.

This incident also casts a spotlight on the complexities of the global pharmaceutical supply chain and manufacturing quality. Annora Pharma Private Limited, based in India, manufactured these tablets for Camber Pharmaceuticals, Inc., a US distributor. Such 'manufactured for' relationships are common, but they necessitate rigorous oversight and communication to ensure product integrity across international borders. A 'foreign tablet' found in a medication bottle points to a breakdown in critical manufacturing processes, such as packaging lines, material segregation, or inspection protocols. It serves as a potent reminder that even with advanced technologies and strict regulations, human error or system failures can occur, demanding continuous vigilance and investment in robust quality assurance systems throughout the entire product lifecycle.

Urgent Alert: Annora Pharma Recalls Lacosamide Tablets Due to Dangerous Foreign Tablet Mix-Up

Published: 2026-06-04  |  Severity: High  |  Source: Original Publication

Article Analysis & Summary

Featured Article on Zero483: A significant pharmaceutical safety alert has emerged from the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA), detailing a Class II recall initiated by Annora Pharma Private Limited. The recall impacts a substantial quantity of Lacosamide Tablets, USP, C V, 100mg, marketed as an Rx-only medication in 60-count bottles. Manufactured by Annora Pharma Pvt., Ltd. in Sangareddy, Telangana, India, for Camber Pharmaceuticals, Inc., Piscataway, NJ 08854, these tablets bear the National Drug Code (NDC) 31722-813-60.

The critical reason behind this nationwide recall is the alarming “presence of a foreign tablet.” Annora Pharma received a complaint indicating a possible mix-up, specifically identifying a Selexipag 1000 mcg tablet within a bottle intended solely for Lacosamide 100mg. This type of error, where an entirely different medication is found co-mingled with or substituted for the intended product, represents a severe breakdown in manufacturing control and poses immediate and potentially life-threatening risks to patients.

Lacosamide is an anti-epileptic drug (AED) crucial for managing partial-onset seizures, while Selexipag is a potent medication used to treat Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension (PAH), a serious and progressive lung condition. The accidental ingestion of Selexipag by a patient expecting Lacosamide could lead to significant adverse cardiac effects, including hypotension, dizziness, and other cardiovascular complications, especially in individuals not suffering from PAH. Conversely, an epileptic patient unknowingly missing their Lacosamide dose by consuming Selexipag could suffer breakthrough seizures, leading to injury or worse.

The scope of this recall is considerable, affecting 31,392 60-count bottles distributed across the entire United States. The FDA has classified this as a Class II recall, meaning that exposure to the violative product may cause temporary or medically reversible adverse health consequences, or where the probability of serious adverse health consequences is remote. However, for vulnerable patient populations relying on precise medication for critical conditions like epilepsy, even a 'temporary' adverse event can have profound and lasting impacts. The recall status is 'ongoing,' indicating that the process of identifying, retrieving, and properly disposing of the affected products is still actively underway.

This incident underscores the paramount importance of stringent quality control throughout the pharmaceutical manufacturing and packaging process. Such mix-ups not only endanger patients but also erode public trust in drug safety and supply chain integrity. Patients and healthcare providers are urged to exercise extreme caution and verify their medications.

Context & Background

Lacosamide, marketed under various brand names, is a well-established antiepileptic drug (AED) pivotal in the management of partial-onset seizures in adults and children. For individuals living with epilepsy, consistent and accurate dosing of their medication is non-negotiable. Missing a dose or inadvertently taking the wrong medication can precipitate breakthrough seizures, leading to physical injury, loss of consciousness, and significant disruption to daily life. The therapeutic window for AEDs is often narrow, and any deviation from the prescribed regimen carries substantial risks to patient stability and quality of life.

In stark contrast, Selexipag is a highly specific and potent medication prescribed for Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension (PAH), a rare, progressive, and life-threatening condition characterized by abnormally high blood pressure in the arteries leading to the lungs. Selexipag works by targeting prostacyclin receptors to relax blood vessels and reduce pressure, thereby improving exercise capacity and delaying disease progression. Its pharmacologic effects, including vasodilation, are significant and can lead to adverse events like headache, jaw pain, nausea, and more seriously, hypotension, especially in individuals not requiring its vasodilatory actions. The profound difference in mechanism of action and intended therapeutic effect between Lacosamide and Selexipag highlights the extreme danger of their accidental mix-up.

This recall, driven by the discovery of a Selexipag tablet within a Lacosamide bottle, reveals a severe breach in pharmaceutical manufacturing quality control. Such foreign tablet contaminations are among the most serious types of deviations because they bypass multiple layers of expected safety checks, including visual inspection, automated sorting, and packaging integrity. The implications extend beyond the immediate patient risk; they challenge the fundamental trust placed in pharmaceutical companies to deliver safe and accurately labeled medications. This incident points to potential systemic issues within the manufacturing facility, requiring a thorough investigation into batch control, line clearance procedures, and automated inspection systems.

For the pharmaceutical industry, this event serves as a critical reminder of the continuous need for robust Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP). Ensuring the right drug is in the right container at the right strength is a bedrock principle. Failures like this not only tarnish a company's reputation but also invite intense regulatory scrutiny from agencies like the FDA, potentially leading to warning letters, import alerts, further inspections, and significant financial penalties. The ripple effect can impact supply chains, patient access, and overall market confidence in drug products originating from affected facilities.

Key Implications & Public Impact

  • Immediate Patient Safety Risk: The most critical impact is the potential for patients with epilepsy to inadvertently take Selexipag instead of Lacosamide, leading to adverse cardiovascular events, or to miss their essential seizure medication, resulting in dangerous breakthrough seizures.
  • Erosion of Public Trust and Manufacturer Reputation: This incident significantly damages the reputation of Annora Pharma and Camber Pharmaceuticals, eroding patient and healthcare provider trust in the safety and quality of their products. This can have long-term market implications.
  • Increased Regulatory Scrutiny: The FDA is likely to intensify inspections and oversight of Annora Pharma's manufacturing facilities, potentially leading to further enforcement actions, import alerts, or delays in future product approvals.
  • Significant Financial and Operational Burden: Recalling over 31,000 bottles incurs substantial costs related to logistics, destruction of product, public notification, and potentially legal liabilities. It also demands extensive internal investigations and corrective actions.
  • Disruption to Healthcare Providers and Supply Chain: Pharmacists and healthcare providers face the burden of identifying affected patients, providing guidance, and managing potential clinical consequences. The recall may also cause temporary shortages of Lacosamide, impacting patient access to critical medication.
Key Takeaways & Recommended Actions:
  • For Patients and Caregivers: Immediately check your Lacosamide 100mg bottles for the NDC (31722-813-60). Carefully inspect the tablets in your bottle; Lacosamide 100mg tablets are typically specific in appearance. If you notice any foreign tablets, or if a tablet's appearance differs from what you expect, do NOT consume it. Contact your prescribing physician or pharmacist immediately for guidance on how to proceed and obtain a replacement.
  • For Pharmacists and Healthcare Providers: Quarantine and cease distribution of any affected Lacosamide 100mg lots (NDC 31722-813-60) from Annora Pharma/Camber Pharmaceuticals. Proactively identify and notify patients who may have received the recalled product. Advise them on the risks and guide them on returning the medication and obtaining a safe replacement.
  • For Wholesalers and Distributors: Immediately halt further distribution of the affected Lacosamide 100mg product. Identify and segregate all recalled stock in your inventory. Follow Annora Pharma or Camber Pharmaceuticals' instructions for the return and proper disposition of the product.
  • For Annora Pharma and Camber Pharmaceuticals: Conduct a thorough and expedited root cause analysis to identify exactly how the Selexipag tablets contaminated the Lacosamide bottles. Implement robust corrective and preventive actions across all relevant manufacturing and packaging processes to prevent recurrence, ensuring enhanced quality control measures.

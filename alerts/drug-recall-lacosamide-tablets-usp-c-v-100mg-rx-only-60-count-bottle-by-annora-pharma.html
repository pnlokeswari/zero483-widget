Urgent FDA Recall: Annora Pharma's Lacosamide Tablets Recalled Over Dangerous Mix-Up

Published: 2026-06-04  |  Severity: High  |  Source: Original Publication

Article Analysis & Summary

A critical safety alert has been issued by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) regarding a significant recall impacting patients relying on a vital epilepsy medication. Annora Pharma Private Limited has initiated a nationwide recall of specific lots of its Lacosamide Tablets, USP, C V, 100mg, 60-count bottles. The grave reason behind this Class II recall is the reported presence of a "foreign tablet" – specifically, Selexipag 1000 mcg tablets – mixed into bottles that should contain only Lacosamide.

This mix-up presents a serious health risk, particularly for individuals battling epilepsy. Lacosamide is an anti-epileptic drug (AED) crucial for preventing and managing seizures. For patients with epilepsy, consistent and correct medication is paramount; missing even a single dose or taking the wrong medication can lead to breakthrough seizures, which can result in injury, status epilepticus (a life-threatening prolonged seizure), or other severe health complications. Conversely, Selexipag is a medication prescribed for a completely different and serious condition: pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH), a type of high blood pressure affecting the arteries in the lungs and heart.

The inadvertent consumption of Selexipag by an epilepsy patient would not only fail to control their seizures but could also expose them to the side effects of Selexipag, such as headaches, diarrhea, nausea, and jaw pain, without any therapeutic benefit for their actual condition. This error could also cause delays in proper epilepsy treatment and diagnosis of new symptoms. The recall affects 31,392 bottles distributed nationwide in the USA, highlighting the broad potential impact on patient safety. The situation underscores the immense importance of stringent quality control within pharmaceutical manufacturing and packaging processes to safeguard public health.

Context & Background

Lacosamide, marketed under the brand name Vimpat, is a critical anti-epileptic drug (AED) used to treat partial-onset seizures in patients with epilepsy. Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder characterized by recurrent, unprovoked seizures, affecting millions worldwide. Effective management of epilepsy relies heavily on strict adherence to prescribed medication regimens. Lacosamide works by enhancing the slow inactivation of voltage-gated sodium channels, a unique mechanism that helps stabilize overactive nerve cells in the brain, thereby preventing seizure activity. Its classification as a Schedule V controlled substance in the U.S. reflects its potential for abuse, though lower than other scheduled drugs, necessitating careful handling and prescription practices.

The discovery of a foreign tablet – Selexipag – in Lacosamide bottles points to a significant breach in the manufacturing and packaging integrity. Selexipag, sold under the brand name Uptravi, belongs to a class of drugs known as prostacyclin receptor agonists and is used exclusively for the treatment of pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH). The stark difference in therapeutic indications and potential side effects between Lacosamide and Selexipag highlights the critical danger of such a mix-up. This incident underscores the complexities and inherent risks within the global pharmaceutical supply chain, where products manufactured by one company (Annora Pharma in India) are often distributed and marketed by another (Camber Pharmaceuticals, Inc. in the USA).

Such packaging errors, while rare, are a recurring concern in the pharmaceutical industry and often trigger Class II recalls, indicating that while the probability of serious adverse health consequences might be remote, temporary or medically reversible adverse consequences are possible. For a patient managing a chronic condition like epilepsy, receiving incorrect medication can lead to a cascade of negative health outcomes, emphasizing why the FDA and regulatory bodies worldwide maintain stringent oversight on pharmaceutical manufacturing practices to ensure the safety, efficacy, and quality of medicines.

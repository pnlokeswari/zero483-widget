Urgent FDA Recall: Annora Pharma's Lacosamide Tablets Mixed with Selexipag – Patient Safety Alert!

Published: 2026-06-04  |  Severity: High  |  Source: Original Publication

Article Analysis & Summary

A critical patient safety alert has been issued by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) regarding a Class II recall initiated by Annora Pharma Private Limited. The recall specifically targets Lacosamide Tablets, USP, C V, 100mg, in 60-count bottles, distributed nationwide across the USA. The serious concern stems from a complaint reporting the presence of foreign tablets – identified as Selexipag 1000 mcg – mixed within a bottle intended solely for Lacosamide.

Lacosamide, marketed under the brand name Vimpat, is a vital antiepileptic drug (AED) prescribed to patients aged four years and older to control partial-onset seizures. It's also used for primary generalized tonic-clonic seizures. As a Schedule V controlled substance, it plays a crucial role in managing neurological conditions, where consistent dosing is paramount to prevent potentially life-threatening seizure episodes. Patients rely on this medication to stabilize hyperexcitable neuronal membranes and maintain a normal quality of life, making any interruption or substitution incredibly dangerous.

The foreign tablet, Selexipag (commonly known by its brand name Uptravi), is an entirely different and potent medication used to treat pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH). This severe and progressive lung condition involves high blood pressure in the arteries leading to the lungs, and Selexipag works as a prostacyclin receptor agonist to relax and widen these blood vessels. The consequences of accidentally taking Selexipag instead of Lacosamide, or missing a dose of Lacosamide due to this mix-up, are profound. A patient suffering from seizures could experience a recurrence of their condition, while inadvertently taking Selexipag could lead to severe hypotension (dangerously low blood pressure), headaches, nausea, or other serious adverse effects not associated with their neurological condition.

This Class II recall classification by the FDA signifies that exposure to the contaminated product may cause temporary or medically reversible adverse health consequences, or where the probability of serious adverse health consequences is remote. However, for individuals relying on these specific medications, the risk is far from remote. With 31,392 60-count bottles distributed, this recall underscores a serious lapse in manufacturing quality control, demanding immediate attention from patients, healthcare providers, and the broader pharmaceutical industry.

Context & Background

The pharmaceutical industry operates under stringent global regulatory frameworks designed to ensure the safety, efficacy, and quality of medicines. This includes comprehensive Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) that dictate everything from facility design and equipment maintenance to personnel training and quality control checks. The current recall, involving a product manufactured in India (Annora Pharma Pvt., Ltd) for distribution in the USA (Camber Pharmaceuticals, Inc.), highlights the complexities and inherent risks within the global pharmaceutical supply chain. While outsourcing manufacturing offers economic benefits, it necessitates robust oversight across international borders to maintain consistent quality standards.

A mix-up of tablets within a single bottle represents a fundamental breakdown in manufacturing controls. Such incidents can occur due to inadequate segregation of different products during packaging, human error during line clearance or setup, or failures in automated inspection systems. The fact that a potent cardiovascular drug (Selexipag) was found within a container meant for an antiepileptic drug (Lacosamide) points to a significant breach in the established protocols designed to prevent cross-contamination and product mix-ups. This type of error is particularly alarming because it goes beyond a labeling mistake; it involves the physical presence of an incorrect active pharmaceutical ingredient, posing an immediate and direct threat to patient health.

This incident will inevitably trigger a thorough investigation by both Annora Pharma and the FDA. Such investigations typically delve deep into manufacturing processes, training records, equipment validation, and quality systems to identify the root cause and implement corrective actions. For the broader industry, it serves as a stark reminder of the continuous need for vigilance, investment in advanced quality control technologies, and rigorous adherence to GMP standards to safeguard public health and maintain trust in the integrity of the global medicine supply. The reputational and financial ramifications for the companies involved can be substantial, reinforcing the critical importance of proactive quality management.

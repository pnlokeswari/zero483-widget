FDA Alert: IntegraDose Recalls Subpotent Vasopressin Syringes – What This Means for Critical Care

Published: 2026-05-18  |  Severity: High  |  Source: Original Publication

Article Analysis & Summary

The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has issued an important Class II drug recall, spotlighting a critical issue with a life-saving medication. IntegraDose Compounding Services LLC, based in Shoreview, MN, is voluntarily recalling specific lots of their Vasopressin 2 Units/2 mL in 0.9% Sodium Chloride syringes. The core reason behind this nationwide recall is that the product has been identified as a “Subpotent Drug,” meaning it contains less of the active pharmaceutical ingredient than it should, potentially rendering it ineffective when desperately needed.

Vasopressin is not just any medication; it’s a powerful hormone used in emergency and critical care settings to manage extremely low blood pressure, often in life-threatening situations like septic shock or during cardiac arrest. A subpotent dose of such a vital drug can have dire consequences, potentially leading to treatment failure, worsening patient conditions, and even serious adverse health events due to the inadequate therapeutic effect. For a drug intended to stabilize vital signs, the precise and accurate dosage is paramount to patient survival and recovery.

The recall affects 287 units of the ready-to-use syringes (NDC 71139-0190-1) distributed across the U.S. While a Class II recall classification suggests that the use of or exposure to the product may cause temporary or medically reversible adverse health consequences, or that the probability of serious adverse health consequences is remote, the context of Vasopressin’s use elevates the concern. In critical scenarios, even a remote chance of ineffectiveness can be catastrophic. The ongoing status of the recall indicates that authorities and the company are actively working to remove the affected product from the market and assess its full impact.

This incident serves as a stark reminder of the rigorous standards expected in pharmaceutical manufacturing, particularly for compounded sterile products where precision, potency, and sterility are non-negotiable. For healthcare providers, this means a swift review of inventory and careful consideration of patient care; for the public, it reinforces the robust system of drug recalls designed to protect health and safety.

Context & Background

Vasopressin, also known as antidiuretic hormone (ADH), is a naturally occurring peptide hormone produced by the hypothalamus and stored in the posterior pituitary gland. Its primary functions in the human body include regulating water reabsorption in the kidneys to maintain fluid balance and acting as a powerful vasoconstrictor, meaning it narrows blood vessels. In a clinical setting, synthetic vasopressin is a cornerstone treatment in intensive care units and emergency departments. It is critically employed to raise blood pressure in patients suffering from distributive shock, particularly septic shock, where the body's vessels dilate, causing a dangerous drop in blood pressure that can lead to organ failure. It also plays a role in treating certain forms of diabetes insipidus, gastrointestinal hemorrhage, and, historically, in cardiac arrest protocols.

The preparation involved in this recall, Vasopressin 2 Units/2 mL in 0.9% Sodium Chloride in a pre-filled syringe, highlights the convenience and necessity of ready-to-administer sterile products in acute care settings. These formulations are designed for rapid deployment, where every second counts. However, the convenience of pre-filled syringes comes with an expectation of uncompromised quality, including guaranteed potency and sterility. When a drug like vasopressin is found to be subpotent, it fails to deliver the expected therapeutic effect, leaving critically ill patients vulnerable and potentially reversing life-saving interventions. This could mean a patient in septic shock might not achieve the target blood pressure, risking irreversible organ damage or death.

IntegraDose Compounding Services LLC operates within the specialized pharmaceutical sector of compounding. Compounding pharmacies create customized medications for individual patients, often due to specific needs like allergies, unique dosages, or alternative delivery methods not met by commercially available products. While traditional compounding caters to individual prescriptions, facilities like IntegraDose, especially those producing sterile drugs for hospital use, often fall under the category of 503B outsourcing facilities. These facilities are subject to more stringent FDA oversight, akin to conventional pharmaceutical manufacturers, particularly following major public health incidents linked to contaminated compounded drugs. The recall underscores the vital importance of robust quality control and testing, especially for potency, in compounded sterile products, where the margin for error is virtually zero given the vulnerability of the patient population and the critical nature of the drugs.

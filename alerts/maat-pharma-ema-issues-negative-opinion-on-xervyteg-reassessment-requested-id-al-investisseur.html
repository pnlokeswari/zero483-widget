EMA Issues Negative Opinion on MaaT Pharma's Xervyteg: What It Means for Microbiome Therapies and Patients

Published: 2026-06-26  |  Severity: Low  |  Source: Original Publication

Article Analysis & Summary

In a significant development for the burgeoning field of microbiome therapeutics, the European Medicines Agency (EMA) has issued a negative opinion on MaaT Pharma's investigational medicine, Xervyteg (MaaT013). This decision, delivered by the EMA's Committee for Medicinal Products for Human Use (CHMP), recommends against granting marketing authorization for the product, which is designed to prevent acute graft-versus-host disease (aGvHD) in patients undergoing allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT). While not a final rejection, a negative opinion represents a substantial hurdle for the French biotech company and its pioneering efforts in live biotherapeutic products (LBPs).

Xervyteg, a novel microbiome restoration therapy derived from pooled healthy donor stool, aims to re-establish a healthy gut microbiota in patients whose microbiome has been severely disrupted by intensive treatments like chemotherapy and antibiotics. Such disruption is a major risk factor for aGvHD, a severe and often life-threatening complication where the transplanted immune cells attack the recipient's tissues. MaaT Pharma has positioned Xervyteg as a crucial innovation to address this critical unmet medical need, particularly in patients with gut microbiota dysbiosis, who face poorer outcomes.

Upon receiving the CHMP's negative assessment, MaaT Pharma swiftly announced its intention to request a re-examination of the opinion. This procedural step allows the company to formally appeal the CHMP's initial conclusions, providing additional data, clarifications, or scientific arguments to address the committee's concerns. The reassessment process typically involves a detailed review by the CHMP over several months, offering MaaT Pharma a crucial opportunity to persuade regulators of Xervyteg's favorable benefit-risk profile and the robustness of its clinical data.

This regulatory setback underscores the rigorous standards applied to novel therapeutic modalities, particularly complex biological products like live biotherapeutics. The EMA's decision sends a clear signal about the high bar for approval in Europe, demanding compelling evidence of both efficacy and safety, along with robust manufacturing and quality control for such innovative treatments. The outcome of the reassessment will not only determine Xervyteg's fate but will also have broader implications for the regulatory pathway of other microbiome-based therapies striving for market entry.

Context & Background

The development of microbiome-based therapies represents one of the most exciting and rapidly advancing frontiers in modern medicine. Scientists are increasingly recognizing the profound influence of the human gut microbiota on health and disease, linking its composition to conditions ranging from infectious diseases and inflammatory bowel disease to neurological disorders and cancer. Fecal Microbiota Transplantation (FMT), the underlying principle of Xervyteg, has been successfully used for decades, albeit often in an unregulated manner, primarily for recurrent Clostridioides difficile infection (CDI). MaaT Pharma's vision is to industrialize and standardize FMT into a safe, pharmaceutical-grade product, making it accessible and regulated.

Xervyteg, specifically known as MaaT013, is designed to prevent acute graft-versus-host disease (aGvHD), a devastating complication affecting up to 70% of patients who undergo allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) for blood cancers and other severe conditions. In aGvHD, the donor's immune cells recognize the recipient's tissues as foreign and launch an attack, leading to severe damage in organs like the skin, liver, and gut. Current treatments for aGvHD, primarily corticosteroids and immunosuppressants, often have limited efficacy and significant side effects, leaving a substantial unmet medical need for safer and more effective preventive and therapeutic strategies. MaaT Pharma sought to fill this gap by leveraging the power of a restored gut microbiome to modulate immune responses and reduce inflammation.

The regulatory journey for live biotherapeutic products (LBPs) like Xervyteg is inherently complex. Unlike traditional small molecule drugs or even monoclonal antibodies, LBPs involve living organisms, which present unique challenges in terms of characterization, standardization, manufacturing consistency, and long-term safety. Regulators worldwide, including the EMA and the US FDA, are actively working to establish clear guidelines for these innovative products, balancing the need for rigorous scientific evidence with the desire to accelerate access to potentially life-saving therapies. This negative opinion from the EMA, even if subject to reassessment, highlights the stringent requirements for demonstrating a compelling benefit-risk profile and robust control over the entire product lifecycle of a novel, complex biological entity.
